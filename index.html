The Lead

Iran Chooses a Successor. The Day-After Problem Hasn't Changed.

Five days into Operation Epic Fury, the IRGC is installing the slain Supreme Leader's son. The structural question the strikes were never designed to answer is now unavoidable.

Five days into the US-Israel military campaign against Iran, the Islamic Republic is doing something the strike planners had no answer for: choosing a new leader. Iran's Assembly of Experts, under pressure from the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps, was expected Thursday to announce Mojtaba Khamenei, son of the killed Supreme Leader, as successor, according to Iran International. The formal selection requires ratification by the 88-member Assembly, but the IRGC's weight behind Mojtaba appears to have resolved what had been a contested field. He is 55, not a senior jurist, and his elevation violates the spirit of the velayat-e faqih doctrine that requires clerical qualification and scholarly standing. The pick reflects the IRGC's priority: continuity and control, not religious legitimacy.

The war is now in its fifth day. US and Israeli forces have conducted roughly 2,000 strikes since February 28, targeting Iranian nuclear facilities, ballistic missile infrastructure, air defenses, and the country's senior leadership. Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei was killed March 1, the first Supreme Leader killed since the Islamic Republic's founding in 1979. Six US soldiers died when a drone struck a command center in Port Shuaiba, Kuwait. Iran has retaliated: missile strikes hit a residential building in Bahrain and a commercial district in Dubai. Iranian-backed proxy forces in Lebanon and Iraq, significantly degraded since 2024, have not yet mobilized in force.

President Trump, speaking Tuesday, said the campaign would "likely take several weeks." Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth said the operation was "not a so-called regime change war," before adding: "the regime sure did change." That formulation captures the gap between stated goal and operational logic that analysts across the spectrum are now examining. The nuclear timeline justification remains contested: Israeli officials argued Iran could enrich uranium to weapons-grade within two weeks of a decision. The UN's nuclear watchdog said Iran was not days or weeks from having atomic weapons at the time the strikes began.

Primary reporting: Iran International on the IRGC-backed succession; Al Jazeera on succession process; AP and Reuters wire reporting on strike operations and casualties.

China Sets 4.5-5% Growth Target Amid Oil Shock and Global Uncertainty

Premier Li Qiang presented China's annual government report Thursday at the National People's Congress, setting a 2026 GDP growth target of 4.5-5%, a slight reduction from prior years that reflects the prolonged property slump and, now, the oil price shock from the US-Iran conflict. The number matters beyond economics: China's domestic growth calculus shapes how much geopolitical risk Beijing will absorb in supporting anti-Western partners. Iran's status as a third leg of the Russia-China-Iran alignment has just become structurally uncertain. Beijing's response to the new Iranian power structure, including whether it extends recognition to a Mojtaba-led government, will be a signal worth watching.

The Arguments

Can Air Power Alone Produce a Stable Iran?

The question analysts are actually fighting about on Day 5

The facts, from reporting: The US and Israel launched Operation Roaring Lion / Operation Epic Fury on February 28, targeting nuclear facilities, ballistic missile sites, air defenses, and senior Iranian leadership simultaneously. By March 1, Supreme Leader Khamenei was dead: a decapitation strike the US military says took "months, and in some cases, years" of planning. Roughly 2,000 strikes have been conducted through the end of Day 3. Trump has set no public timeline for objectives. The IRGC is intact as an institution, Hezbollah has signaled reluctance to mobilize on Iran's behalf, and Iran has retaliated against Gulf civilian targets. The Strait of Hormuz remains threatened but not closed.

Position 1: The Strategic Logic Holds

Elliott Abrams, senior fellow at the Council on Foreign Relations, argued at a March 2 CFR briefing that the strikes represent a strategic recalculation that prior administrations lacked the will to execute. "What will clearly emerge from this war," Abrams stated, "is a very, very much changed regime": one without a supreme leader truly supreme as before, without a nuclear program, without functioning missile launchers, and without a navy. The cumulative effect is not regime change in the aspirational, democracy-promotion sense, but strategic neutralization of the most dangerous regional actor.

The nuclear timeline argument is the load-bearing logic here. Israeli officials told journalists that Iran could have enriched uranium to 90% (weapons grade) within two weeks of a decision to do so. The window for preventing a nuclear Iran through diplomacy had narrowed to implausibility; the Trump-Netanyahu campaign represents the exhaustion of the alternative. Abrams and like-minded analysts add a second-order argument: a strategically neutered Iran removes a critical node in the Russia-China-Iran anti-Western alignment, potentially slowing the drone and missile supply chains that have sustained Russia's war in Ukraine. Max Boot, who called the war "unwarranted and illegal" at the same CFR briefing, nonetheless acknowledged: "That doesn't necessarily mean it'll be unsuccessful."

Position 2: There Is No Endgame

Gideon Rachman, the Financial Times's chief foreign affairs commentator, wrote March 2 that Trump "has not understood the lessons of past wars for regime change in Iraq and Afghanistan." Rachman's critique is structural, not merely moral: unlike those invasions, where Washington at least deployed ground troops and attempted to construct a successor government, this campaign relies entirely on aviation. If IRGC soldiers decide to lay down their arms, there is, as he puts it, no alternative government or legitimate army to which they can surrender. Trump's calls for Iranians to "take power into their own hands" are, in Rachman's reading, not a strategy but a wish.

Anne Applebaum, writing on her Substack, makes the governance-failure argument with additional historical texture. The campaign was "launched without explanation, without Congress, without even an attempt to build public support, and above all without a coherent strategy for the Iranian people, and without a plan to help them build a legitimate Iranian state." She notes the pattern: on eight occasions during earlier Iranian protests, Trump encouraged Iranians to take over their institutions and promised American help was "on its way." Then his administration sent the opposite signal. Decapitating a 45-year-old ideological system without a theory of what replaces it is not an oversight; it is the plan, and the plan has no second act. Ray Takeyh, the CFR's senior Iran expert, adds the structural counter to the decapitation thesis: "Iran is not a personalized dictatorship. It's an ideological system with a multilayered cadre." You cannot bomb an ideology out of existence.

Position 3: What Neither Side Is Talking About

Adam Tooze, writing in Chartbook 436 published March 4, maps the conflict's most underexamined dimension: its agricultural consequences. The Strait of Hormuz carries roughly 20% of global oil trade and a significant share of the world's liquefied natural gas. Gas price shocks propagate to fertilizer production costs, and fertilizer cost spikes hit sub-Saharan African agrarian economies with disproportionate severity, as Tooze documented during the Ukraine war in countries like Kenya, Nigeria, and Cote d'Ivoire. The populations bearing the severest long-term consequences of a prolonged Hormuz disruption have no seat at any table where this war is being decided.

The second blind spot is theological. The succession crisis is not only a political problem; it is a doctrinal one. The velayat-e faqih principle, clerical guardianship of the state and the ideological core of the Islamic Republic since 1979, requires that the Supreme Leader be a qualified senior jurist with religious standing. Mojtaba Khamenei, installed under IRGC pressure, satisfies neither condition in the eyes of significant portions of Iran's own clerical establishment. Mohsen Kadivar, the dissident Iranian cleric and research professor at Duke University who has spent decades arguing that velayat-e faqih lacks legitimate theological foundation, has not yet written directly on the 2026 succession, but his decades-long framework is exactly the lens through which the regime's credibility crisis must be understood. This is not merely a leadership transition. It is a constitutive crisis for the ideology that has governed 90 million people for 45 years, happening simultaneously with a military campaign that has produced no plan for what any successor ideology might look like.

Person to Know
Mohsen Kadivar

Kadivar is a mujtahid (a qualified Islamic jurist) and research professor of Islamic studies at Duke University, where he has been in exile since 2007. He was trained in the Shia theological seminaries of Qom, served 18 months in an Iranian prison after the Special Clerical Court convicted him in 1999, and has spent three decades building the most rigorous theological case against velayat-e faqih from within the Shia tradition itself. His core argument: the Guardianship of the Jurist is "neither intuitively obvious, nor rationally necessary": not required by the Quran, not established by tradition, not a requirement of religion. That argument was heterodox during Khamenei's lifetime. With an unqualified successor being installed under IRGC pressure, it is now the framework the Islamic Republic's own clergy will be forced to confront. In the coming weeks, watch for who in Iran's clerical establishment invokes Kadivar's framework, even if they don't name him.
