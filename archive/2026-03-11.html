Wednesday, March 11, 2026

Tanker traffic through the Strait of Hormuz has dropped 90 percent. The G7 met twice and did nothing.

The Iran war is now the largest oil supply disruption in recorded history. Tanker traffic through the Strait of Hormuz, which carries roughly 20 percent of the world's oil, fell 90 percent from pre-war levels according to MarineTraffic. Brent crude touched $119.50 a barrel Monday morning before pulling back to around $99 after France's Macron, as G7 chair, said releasing strategic petroleum reserves was "an envisaged option." G7 finance ministers held an emergency call Monday but could not agree on a coordinated release. They punted the decision to energy ministers, who met Tuesday morning. The proposal on the table: a joint release of 300 to 400 million barrels from G7 stockpiles, roughly a quarter of total reserves.

The delay matters because the disruption is structural, not speculative. Iran's IRGC has warned it will attack any vessel transiting the strait. Insurers have pulled war-risk coverage from the Gulf. Even if no tanker is hit, the insurance gap alone keeps ships from sailing. Trump floated two responses: Navy escorts for tankers, and a vague suggestion that the United States might "take over" the strait entirely. Neither has materialized. The IEA formally requested the coordinated reserve release during the G7 call, an unusual step that signals the agency views the supply gap as severe enough to threaten global economic stability.

Markets swung wildly. Japan's Nikkei fell more than 5 percent. South Korea's KOSPI dropped 6 percent. European indexes opened sharply lower. Then Wall Street staged a late-session recovery on hopes that Trump's ceasefire rhetoric and the G7 reserves discussion signaled the worst was passing. It may not be. Iran's foreign minister repeated that his country will not negotiate while under attack. France, China, and Russia have all reached out to Tehran about ceasefire terms, but there is no framework, no mediator with standing on both sides, and no sign that Trump's demand for unconditional surrender has changed. The war is twelve days old.
