Iran is keeping the Strait of Hormuz closed. Oil surged back above $100. The IEA's record reserve release isn't enough.

The $34 oil crash that followed Trump's "very complete" rhetoric and Energy Secretary Wright's deleted tanker-escort post lasted three days. Brent crude climbed back above $101 a barrel Thursday after Iran attacked three more commercial ships near the strait, including a Thai bulk carrier that caught fire with three crew still missing, a Japanese container vessel, and a Marshall Islands-flagged carrier hit northwest of Dubai. Two drones fell near Dubai International Airport, injuring four people. Tehran declared it would not permit "even a single liter of oil" through the waterway and warned it would begin targeting US-linked banks across the Middle East. The strait is not just closed by mines and insurance gaps. Iran is now actively hunting commercial shipping.

The IEA's 32 member nations unanimously approved a 400-million-barrel strategic reserve release on Wednesday, the largest in the agency's history, more than double the 182 million barrels released during Russia's invasion of Ukraine. The reserves buy roughly 60 to 90 days of lost Hormuz flow. That math only works if the strait reopens within that window. The ship attacks and Iran's explicit promise to keep it shut suggest it won't. Morgan Stanley, BlackRock, and Blackstone have all capped withdrawals from private credit funds this month as the oil shock spreads into the financial system. Morgan Stanley's $8 billion North Haven fund honored less than half of investor redemption requests. The economic damage is no longer confined to energy prices.

Trump and Netanyahu are now publicly diverging on the war's timeline. Trump told reporters there is "practically nothing left" to bomb and that he can end the war whenever he wants. Israeli Defense Minister Katz said operations will continue "without any time limit." Trump acknowledged to The Times of Israel that ending the war would be a "mutual decision" with Netanyahu, giving Israel an effective veto on when the US stops fighting. Meanwhile, a Pentagon investigation found the US likely struck a girls' school in Minab on the war's first night using an AI-generated target list built on 2013 satellite imagery, killing 175 people, most of them children. The school had been visibly separated from an adjacent military compound since 2016. The war is two weeks old. The strait is closed, the reserves are draining, and no one has agreed on how to stop.
