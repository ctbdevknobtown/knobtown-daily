Saudi Arabia shut down two supergiant oil fields. There's nowhere to send the oil.

Saudi Aramco closed the Safaniya and Zuluf offshore fields this week, cutting the kingdom's output by roughly 2 million barrels a day to around 8 million. Safaniya is the world's largest offshore oil field. The reason for the shutdown is not damage or sanctions. It is storage. With the Strait of Hormuz effectively closed to commercial tanker traffic for two weeks, Gulf producers have been filling onshore tanks with crude that has no way out. Saudi Arabia has tried to reroute some exports through its East-West pipeline to the Red Sea port of Yanbu, but the system carries lighter crude grades and cannot handle the heavy oil from Safaniya and Zuluf. The physics are simple: if you can't ship it, you can't pump it.

Iran's new supreme leader, Mojtaba Khamenei, made his first major policy statement Thursday by pledging to maintain the strait closure indefinitely. Iran attacked two more oil tankers in Iraqi territorial waters, setting both ablaze; 38 crew members were rescued by Iraqi port authorities. Brent crude held above $101. The IEA estimates the war has removed roughly 8 million barrels a day from global supply, the largest sudden disruption the oil market has ever absorbed. The 400-million-barrel reserve release, approved unanimously this week, covers about 50 days at that rate. CNBC's analysis was blunt: the United States has exhausted its economic tools for bringing oil prices down. The only remaining option is military reopening of the strait.

That option is getting harder. Iran has mined the waterway, attacked commercial shipping, and demonstrated it can hit targets in Iraqi, Emirati, and even Turkish territory. Two Iranian ballistic missiles have entered NATO airspace in the past ten days. The US has destroyed more than 5,000 targets in Iran, but Trump told Axios there is "practically nothing left" to hit, and the Pentagon has not launched any operation to clear the strait or escort tankers through it. The war has entered a phase where the bombing is winding down but the economic weapon Iran built is getting stronger. Every day the strait stays closed, more fields shut in, more reserves drain, and the window for a manageable exit narrows.
