The IEA proposed releasing 400 million barrels of oil, the largest reserve drawdown in history. The strait is still mined.

The International Energy Agency on Wednesday proposed the largest coordinated release of strategic oil reserves ever attempted: 400 million barrels, to be drawn from stockpiles across 32 member nations over 60 to 90 days. The previous record was the 2022 response to Russia's invasion of Ukraine, which totaled 182 million barrels. This proposal is more than double that. The IEA's member countries were expected to vote Wednesday, and under the agency's rules, the release proceeds unless a single country objects. Brent crude held steady near $88 a barrel on the news, down from Monday's $119 peak but still roughly 40 percent above pre-war levels.

The scale of the release reflects the scale of the problem. The Strait of Hormuz, through which a fifth of the world's oil flows, is now physically blocked. Iran laid naval mines in the waterway this week. CENTCOM destroyed 16 minelaying vessels on Tuesday, but mines already in the water remain. Even after a ceasefire, clearing them could take weeks. Insurers have pulled war-risk coverage. No commercial tanker is willing to transit. The IEA's 400 million barrels would replace roughly two months of lost Hormuz flow, buying time but not solving the structural crisis. If the strait stays closed past May, the reserves run out and the world is back where it started, except with empty stockpiles.

Iran, meanwhile, named its conditions for talks: a full nuclear fuel cycle, compensation for war damages, and guarantees against future attack. These are broader demands than what was on the table before the February negotiations collapsed, which means Iran's position has hardened, not softened, after thirteen days of bombing. Russia submitted a draft UN Security Council resolution calling for an immediate ceasefire; the US is expected to veto it. China's stock market, notably, has held up better than any other major index since the war began, a fact Bloomberg attributed to Beijing's long-term oil contracts with Iran that bypass the strait entirely. The war is two weeks old. The emergency systems are activated. None of them are designed to last.
