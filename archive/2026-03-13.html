The Energy Secretary said the Navy escorted a tanker through Hormuz. It hadn't. Oil crashed 15 percent anyway.

Energy Secretary Chris Wright posted Tuesday that the US Navy had successfully escorted an oil tanker through the Strait of Hormuz. Within hours, oil dropped more than 15 percent, Brent crude falling from $99 to $85. It was the largest single-day oil crash in four years. Then the White House corrected him. Press Secretary Karoline Leavitt said the Navy had not escorted any tanker or vessel. An Energy Department spokesperson said the post was "incorrectly captioned by Department of Energy staff." Wright deleted it. Joint Chiefs Chairman Gen. Dan Caine confirmed at a Pentagon briefing that no escort operations had begun. Oil stayed down.

What actually happened at the strait was worse than the fiction. CNN reported that Iran had begun laying naval mines in the waterway. CENTCOM said it destroyed 16 Iranian minelaying vessels Tuesday, the most direct naval engagement of the war. Mining the strait is an act of desperation and escalation: mines are indiscriminate, nearly impossible to fully clear, and would shut down the waterway for weeks even after a ceasefire. Insurers were already refusing war-risk coverage for Gulf shipping. Confirmed mines would freeze the last trickle of tanker traffic that remained. The 15 percent oil crash was built on a lie about safe passage through a waterway that Iran was actively filling with explosives.

The market moved on the words, not the facts. Oil went from $119 on Monday morning to $85 by Tuesday afternoon, a $34 swing in 36 hours, driven by Trump's "very complete" rhetoric, the G7's vague promise to release reserves, and a cabinet secretary's false post. The underlying reality has not changed: the strait is mined, Iran's foreign minister has rejected every ceasefire proposal, and the US flew its most intense bombing sorties of the war on the same day its president said it was almost over. More than 1,700 people are now dead across the region. The war is twelve days old and the price of oil is being set by deleted tweets.
